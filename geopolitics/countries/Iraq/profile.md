### Mohammed Shia' al-Sudani (Prime Minister)
- **Born:** 1970 in Maysan Province, Iraq
- **Background:** Shia politician, former Minister of Human Rights (2010-2014), former Governor of Maysan Province
- **Political Affiliation:** Independent, backed by Shia political coalition
- **Key Policy Positions:**
  - Pursues "policy of friendship and cooperation with everyone" - rejects alignment with any single bloc
  - Emphasizes Iraq should not be "a base from which other countries are attacked"
  - Seeks foreign investment for reconstruction and economic development
  - Balances relations between Western allies and Iranian neighbors

### Political System
- **Type:** Federal parliamentary republic with multi-party system
- **Legislature:** Council of Representatives (329 seats)
- **Constitution:** Adopted October 2005
- **Key Political Forces:**
  - Shia political parties (largest bloc, including Sadrist Movement, State of Law Coalition)
  - Kurdish parties (KDP, PUK) controlling Kurdistan Regional Government
  - Sunni Arab political coalitions
  - Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF/Hashd al-Shaabi) - state-sponsored paramilitary with political influence

### Governance Challenges
- Persistent corruption and bureaucratic inefficiency
- Ethnic and sectarian power-sharing disputes
- Weak institutional capacity
- Ongoing tensions between federal government and Kurdistan Regional Government (KRG)
- Influence of Iran-backed militias within state apparatus

## 3. Foreign Relations

### Strategic Positioning
Iraq occupies a unique position in Middle Eastern geopolitics, attempting to balance relationships between regional rivals while maintaining sovereignty. The Sudani government explicitly rejects the "policy of axes," seeking cooperative relations with all parties.

### Relations with the United States
- **Historical Context:** Hostile relations 1990-2003; occupation 2003-2011; coalition against ISIS 2014-2021
- **Current Status:** Strategic partnership; approximately 2,500 US troops remain for counter-terrorism advisory role
- **Key Agreements:** U.S.-Iraq Strategic Framework Agreement (2008)
- **Tensions:** 
  - Iranian-backed militias target US forces periodically
  - Political pressure for US withdrawal
  - September 2024: Agreement reached for partial US troop withdrawal within ~1 year
- **Economic Ties:** Major trade partner; significant oil exports to US

### Relations with Iran
- **Historical Context:** Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988); adversarial under Saddam; close ties post-2003
- **Current Status:** Deep but complex relationship
  - Iran wields significant political influence through Shia political parties
  - Economic interdependence: Iran supplies electricity and natural gas
  - Iranian-backed militias (Kataib Hezbollah, Asaib Ahl al-Haq) operate within Popular Mobilization Forces
- **Key Issues:**
  - Water rights (Shatt al-Arab dispute resolved by 1975 Algiers Agreement)
  - Iranian influence over Iraqi politics
  - Cross-border militia activities
  - Baghdad-Damascus-Beirut "Shia corridor" disrupted by fall of Assad in Syria (2024)

### Relations with Turkey
- **Historical Context:** Complex history; Ottoman rule until WWI; disputes over Kurdish question
- **Current Status:** Economic partnership but tensions persist
- **Key Issues:**
  - Turkish military operations against PKK in northern Iraq
  - Water rights (Tigris-Euphrates river management)
  - "Iraq Development Road" project (2023) - $17 billion rail/road corridor connecting Persian Gulf to Turkey
  - KRG-Turkey oil pipeline (independent of Baghdad)
  - Turkish incursions into Kurdistan region

### Relations with Saudi Arabia
- **Historical Context:** Adversarial during Saddam era; restoration of relations in 2015
- **Current Status:** Growing cooperation and investment
- **Key Developments:**
  - Saudi Arabia reopened embassy in Baghdad (2015)
  - Investment pledges for reconstruction
  - Iraqi mediation between Saudi Arabia and Iran (2021-2023)
  - Saudi investment in Iraqi infrastructure and energy sectors

### Relations with Kuwait
- **Historical Context:** Iraqi invasion (1990) led to Gulf War; reparations paid
- **Current Status:** Normalized relations
- **Key Issues:**
  - Remaining reparations from 1990 invasion
  - Border demarcation
  - Port access and shared oil fields

### Relations with Arab Gulf States (UAE, Qatar)
- **Current Status:** Significant economic engagement
- **Key Developments:**
  - UAE and Qatar signed memoranda of understanding for "Iraq Development Road" project (April 2024)
  - Gulf states investing in Iraqi infrastructure and development
  - Economic diversification away from Iranian dependence

### Relations with Russia
- **Historical Context:** Soviet ally during Cold War; arms supplier
- **Current Status:** Limited but present engagement
- **Key Ties:** Arms purchases; energy sector cooperation
- **Impact of Ukraine War:** Russia's diminished regional influence

### Relations with China
- **Status:** Growing economic presence
- **Key Areas:** Oil sector investment; infrastructure development
- **Belt and Road:** Iraq is part of China's Belt and Road Initiative

## 4. Military Capabilities

### Overview
Iraq's military has been rebuilt since the 2003 dissolution of Saddam's forces and the war against ISIS (2014-2017). The armed forces face significant challenges including corruption, sectarian influence, and militia integration issues.

### Iraqi Armed Forces (Al-Quwwat al-Musallaha al-Iraqiyya)

| Branch | Personnel | Key Equipment |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Army (Ground Forces)** | ~193,000 active | M1A1 Abrams tanks, M2/M3 Bradley IFVs, T-72 tanks, BMP-1 IFVs |
| **Air Force** | ~5,000 | F-16IQ fighters, C-130 transport, Mi-28 attack helicopters |
| **Navy** | ~5,000 | Coastal patrol vessels, marine battalions |
| **Air Defence Command** | ~10,000 | SAM systems, radar networks |
| **Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF)** | ~150,000+ | Various; includes Iranian-backed militias |

### Military Budget
- **2023:** $10.3 billion (approximately 3.8% of GDP)
- **Major Suppliers:** United States, Russia, China, Iran, Turkey, South Korea, Germany, France

### Key Military Assets

#### Conventional Forces
- **Armor:** M1A1 Abrams (approximately 140+), T-72 variants, M60 tanks
- **Infantry Fighting Vehicles:** M2/M3 Bradley, BMP-1/2
- **Artillery:** Various towed and self-propelled systems
- **Air Force:** 36 F-16IQ block 52 fighters (US-supplied), attack helicopters

#### Strategic Capabilities
- **No Nuclear Weapons:** Iraq abandoned its nuclear program in 1991; facilities destroyed
- **No Chemical/Biological Weapons:** All destroyed post-1991 Gulf War; inspections completed 2003
- **Missile Capability:** Limited short-range ballistic missiles; no significant missile development program

### Popular Mobilization Forces (Hashd al-Shaabi)
- Established 2014 to fight ISIS
- State-sponsored paramilitary organization
- Approximately 150,000+ fighters across ~50 brigades
- **Controversial Elements:** Iranian-backed brigades (Kataib Hezbollah, Asaib Ahl al-Haq) with significant autonomy
- **Political Influence:** PMF has representation in parliament and government ministries

### Kurdish Forces (Peshmerga)
- **Status:** Separate from Iraqi Armed Forces; loyal to Kurdistan Regional Government
- **Strength:** Estimated 150,000-200,000 fighters
- **Organization:** Split between KDP (Barzani) and PUK (Talabani) factions
- **Equipment:** Mix of Soviet-era and Western equipment; light infantry focus
- **Relation to Baghdad:** Constitutional status disputed; integration negotiations ongoing

### Security Challenges
- ISIS insurgency remnants in rural areas
- Iranian-backed militia attacks on US forces
- Intra-militia violence
- Corruption and desertion
- Limited air defense capabilities
- Logistical and maintenance dependencies on foreign support

## 5. Economy & Resources

### Overview
Iraq's economy is heavily dependent on oil, accounting for over 90% of government revenue and 95% of foreign exchange earnings. Post-war reconstruction and diversification remain key government priorities.

### Oil Sector

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| **Proven Oil Reserves** | 145 billion barrels (5th largest globally) |
| **Daily Production** | 4.2-4.5 million barrels/day (2024) |
| **OPEC Quota** | ~4.4 million b/d |
| **Key Oil Fields** | Rumaila, West Qurna, Majnoon, Zubair, Kirkuk |

### Natural Gas
- **Reserves:** 3.7 trillion cubic meters (12th globally)
- **Development Status:** Limited; significant flaring of associated gas
- **Projects:** Gas processing infrastructure under development

### Non-Oil Economy
- **Agriculture:** Dates, wheat, barley, cotton; limited by water scarcity
- **Industry:** Petrochemicals, construction materials, textiles
- **Services:** Growing telecommunications and banking sectors

### Key Trade Partners
- **Exports:** China (largest), India, United States, South Korea
- **Imports:** China, Turkey, Iran, United Arab Emirates

### Economic Challenges
- Over-reliance on oil revenues
- Corruption and inefficiency
- Infrastructure damage from decades of conflict
- Unemployment (approximately 13-16% nationally; higher among youth)
- Water scarcity and climate change impacts
- Electricity shortages (dependency on Iranian imports)

### Infrastructure Projects
- **Iraq Development Road (2023-):** $17 billion rail and highway corridor from Persian Gulf port of al-Faw to Turkey, connecting to European markets
- **Basra-Shalamcheh Railway:** Connecting to Iranian rail network
- **Port Development:** Al-Faw Grand Port construction

## 7. Historical Context

### Ottoman Empire to British Mandate (1918-1932)
- Part of Ottoman Empire until World War I
- British mandate established 1920; Kingdom of Iraq created 1921 under Hashemite monarchy
- Independence achieved 1932; British influence persisted

### Hashemite Monarchy (1932-1958)
- Pro-Western monarchy under King Faisal I, then Ghazi, then Faisal II
- 1941 Anglo-Iraqi War: British intervention against pro-Axis coup
- Baghdad Pact (1955): Alliance with Western powers

### Republican Era Beginnings (1958-1968)
- **July 14, 1958 Revolution:** Coup overthrew monarchy; Abd al-Karim Qasim came to power
- Withdrawal from Baghdad Pact; Soviet alignment
- 1963 Ba'athist coup; short-lived
- 1968: Ba'ath Party seized power permanently

### Ba'athist Iraq under Saddam Hussein (1979-2003)

#### Saddam Hussein Regide
- **Rise to Power:** Vice president under Ahmed Hassan al-Bakr; assumed presidency July 1979
- **Purges:** Systematic elimination of rivals; totalitarian control
- **Economic Development:** Oil nationalization (1972); infrastructure modernization; high literacy rates
- **Human Rights:** 250,000-290,000 Iraqis killed or disappeared; torture, ethnic cleansing

#### Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988)
- Launched by Saddam September 1980
- Stalemate; estimated 500,000+ deaths combined
- Western support for Iraq (US, France, UK, Soviet Union)
- Use of chemical weapons against Iran and Kurdish civilians (Halabja 1988)

#### Gulf War (1990-1991)
- Invasion of Kuwait August 1990
- US-led coalition expelled Iraqi forces February 1991
- UN sanctions imposed; devastating economic impact
- "Oil for Food" program (1996-2003)

#### 2003 Invasion and Regime Change
- US-led coalition invaded March 2003
- Baghdad fell April 9, 2003
- Saddam captured December 2003; executed December 2006
- Dissolution of Iraqi Armed Forces (CPA Order 2, May 2003)

### Post-2003 Era

#### Insurgency and Civil War (2003-2011)
- Sunni insurgency against occupation
- Sectarian violence (2006-2008 peak)
- "Surge" strategy 2007-2008 reduced violence
- US withdrawal completed December 2011

#### ISIS War (2014-2017)
- **June 2014:** ISIS captured Mosul; declared caliphate
- Iraqi forces collapsed; Kurdish forces took Kirkuk
- International coalition formed; US-led air campaign
- Iraqi forces regained territory 2015-2017
- **December 2017:** Iraqi government declared victory over ISIS

#### Reconstruction Period (2018-present)
- Post-ISIS governance challenges
- Protest movements (2019-2020): Demanded economic reform, anti-corruption
- Government formation crises
- Infrastructure reconstruction
- Balancing Iran and Western influences

## 8. Current Priorities & Strategy 2025-2026

### Government Priorities

#### 1. Economic Reconstruction & Diversification
- Reduce oil dependency through industrial development
- Attract foreign investment in infrastructure
- Combat corruption in oil revenue management
- Implement "Iraq Development Road" corridor project
- Expand electricity generation capacity

#### 2. Security Stabilization
- Counter ISIS remnants in rural areas
- Integrate Popular Mobilization Forces into formal security apparatus
- Balance US military presence with Iranian-backed militia pressures
- Address Kurdish autonomy and oil revenue disputes

#### 3. Regional Diplomatic Balance
- Maintain cooperative relations with Iran while developing ties to Arab states
- Host regional dialogue initiatives
- Manage impact of Syrian regime change (Assad's fall, December 2024)
- Navigate US pressure to distance from Iranian influence

#### 4. Internal Governance
- Combat corruption across government institutions
- Reform electoral system
- Address public service delivery (electricity, water, healthcare)
- Manage ethnic and sectarian tensions

#### 5. Infrastructure Development
- Complete "Development Road" railway/highway corridor
- Expand port capacity at al-Faw
- Modernize electricity grid
- Improve water infrastructure (Tigris-Euphrates management)

### Strategic Challenges

1. **Iranian Influence:** Iranian-backed militias within PMF complicate sovereignty
2. **US-Iranian Tensions:** Iraq caught between competing powers
3. **Kurdistan Relations:** KRG oil exports independent of Baghdad; status disputes
4. **Syrian Situation:** Fall of Assad (December 2024) disrupts Tehran-Baghdad-Damascus corridor
5. **Water Crisis:** Climate change and upstream dam projects (Turkey, Iran) threaten water security
6. **Economic Dependency:** Oil price volatility; need for diversification
