## 2. Leadership & Politics

### Kim Jong-un (김정은)
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Title** | Supreme Leader, First Secretary of WPK, Chairman of State Affairs Commission |
| **Born** | January 8, 1984 (est.) |
| **In Power** | Since December 17, 2011 (father Kim Jong-il's death) |
| **Dynasty** | Third generation of Kim family rule (grandfather Kim Il-sung founded DPRK in 1948) |
| **Succession** | Daughter Kim Ju-ae (born ~2013) visible in state media; son reportedly exists but never shown |

### Power Structure

**The Kim Dynasty** operates as a de facto monarchy under the guise of socialist republicanism:

1. **Kim Jong-un** — Supreme Leader; final authority on all decisions
2. **Workers' Party of Korea (WPK)** — Sole legal party; controls state apparatus
3. **Korean People's Army (KPA)** — 1.2+ million active personnel; "Songun" (military-first) policy prioritizes military access to resources
4. **Cabinet** — Administrative body; implements party decisions with limited autonomy

**Key Figures (2024-2025):**
- **Kim Yo-jong** — Sister of Kim Jong-un; powerful propaganda chief, potential successor
- **Choe Ryong-hae** — Chairman of Standing Committee of Supreme People's Assembly
- **Pak Jong-chon** — Chief of General Staff (military)
- **Ri Yong-ho** — Former foreign minister; purged 2022

**Ideology:**
- **Juche (自主体)** — Self-reliance doctrine established by Kim Il-sung; core state ideology
- **Songun (先军)** — "Military-first" policy prioritizes military access to resources (Kim Jong-il era, continues)
- **Byungjin (並進)** — "Parallel development" of nuclear weapons and economy (Kim Jong-un doctrine)

### Political Purges

Kim Jong-un has conducted extensive purges to consolidate power:
- **Jang Song-thaek** (2013) — Uncle, executed; faction elimination signal
- **Ri Yong-ho** (2022) — Foreign minister; replaced after failed diplomacy
- Estimated **300+ senior officials** purged or executed since 2011
- Regular leadership reshuffles prevent challenge formation

| Mineral | Reserves | Global Significance |

### Energy

| Source | Percentage |
|--------|------------|
| **Coal** | Primary domestic energy source |
| **Hydroelectric** | ~50% of electricity generation |
| **Oil** | Imported from China; tightly rationed |
| **Natural Gas** | Negligible |

**Energy Crisis:** Chronic electricity shortages; Pyongyang prioritized; rural areas have <6 hours/day.

### Sanctions Impact

**UN Security Council Sanctions (2006-2024):**
- Cap on oil imports (4.4M barrels/year)
- Ban on coal, iron, seafood, textile exports
- Ban on overseas labor
- Asset freezes and travel bans on individuals/entities
- Inspection of cargo to/from DPRK

**Impact:** Economy contracted but survived via:
- China trade (sanctions evasion)
- Cyber theft (Bureau 121)
- Shadow shipping fleet
- Diplomatic smuggling

### Marketization

Despite command economy, markets have emerged:
- **Jangmadang** — Unofficial markets selling food, goods, currency exchange
- **Private Enterprise** — De facto private businesses with state "permission"
- **Foreign Currency** | USD, EUR, CNY widely used for transactions

### Korean War (1950-1953)

| Fact | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Started** | June 25, 1950 — North Korean invasion of South |
| **Participants** | DPRK + China + USSR vs. ROK + US + UN Command (16 nations) |
| **Casualties** | ~3 million total (military + civilian) |
| **Outcome** | Armistice signed July 27, 1953; no peace treaty |
| **Status** | Still technically at war; DMZ established |
| **US Troops** | 28,500 stationed in South Korea since 1953 |

### Division Legacy

- **DMZ:** 4km-wide, 250km-long demilitarized zone; most heavily fortified border on Earth
- **Separated Families:** ~10 million Koreans separated from family members since division
- **No Treaty:** Only armistice; war legally ongoing
- **Nuclear Issue:** Direct consequence of unresolved war and security dilemma

## 8. Current Priorities & Strategy 2025-2026

### Regime Survival Priority

**Kim Jong-un's primary goal is regime survival.** Nuclear weapons are the ultimate deterrent against foreign intervention (Libya/Iraq model).

### Strategic Objectives (2025-2026)

| Priority | Strategy |
|----------|----------|
| **Nuclear Deterrence** | Maintain nuclear arsenal; develop second-strike capability; tactical nukes for South Korea |
| **Russia Alignment** | Arms-for-resources deal; troops in Ukraine; technology/economic exchange |
| **Economic Recovery** | Post-COVID reopening; maintain Chinese lifeline; limited marketization |
| **South Korea Hostility** | Permanent enemy designation; abandon unification goal; justify military spending |
| **US Engagement** | Keep door open for summit diplomacy; demand sanctions relief without denuclearization |
| **Internal Control** | Information quarantine; elite loyalty maintenance; prevent dissent |

### Nuclear Doctrine (2024-2025)

**Official Policy:** Nuclear forces are for deterrence, but first-use doctrine articulated:

1. **First Use:** Permitted if regime survival threatened
2. **Tactical Nukes:** Deployed for use against South Korea
3. **ICBM Development:** Continue improving range, reliability, MIRV capability
4. **SSBN Program:** Submarine-launched ballistic missiles for second-strike
5. **Nuclear Law (2022):** Codifies nuclear state status; automatic use if attacked

### Russia Partnership (2024-2025)

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Arms Transfers** | Artillery shells, missiles to Russia for Ukraine war |
| **Troop Deployment** | 10,000+ DPRK troops in Russia (Kursk region, 2024-2025) |

| **Strategic Benefit** | Breaks international isolation; gains combat experience; economic lifeline |
