# Russia - Geopolitical Profile 2025

## 2. Leadership & Politics

### Current Leadership Structure

**President Vladimir Putin** has dominated Russian politics since 1999, first as Prime Minister, then President from 2000-2008, again from 2012-present. His rule has transitioned from a nominally democratic system to an increasingly authoritarian personalist regime.

**Key Power Centers:**
- **Siloviki Network:** Security services veterans, particularly FSB, form the core of Putin's inner circle
- **Oligarchs:** Business elites who maintain influence through proximity to Putin
- **State Corporations:** State-controlled enterprises in energy, defense, and finance serve as instruments of political control

### Political System

Russia operates as a **federal semi-presidential republic** in name, but functions as an **authoritarian personalist regime**:

- **United Russia party** holds dominant control of the State Duma (lower house)
- **Opposition parties** exist within strictly controlled limits
- **Federal Assembly:** Bicameral legislature (Federation Council + State Duma) with limited independent authority
- **Judiciary:** Subordinate to executive branch; Constitutional Court and Supreme Court operate under political constraints

### Domestic Political Dynamics

- **2024 Presidential Election:** Putin won with reported ~87% of vote in elections widely criticized as unfree
- **Constitutional Changes (2020):** Reset presidential term limits, allowing Putin to potentially rule until 2036
- **Censorship & Control:** Extensive media control, criminalization of dissent, designation of opposition figures as "foreign agents" or "extremists"
- **Political Repression:** Imprisonment of opposition figures (Alexei Navalny imprisoned 2021-2024, died in custody February 2024); exile of opposition leaders

### Foreign Policy Doctrines

Russian foreign policy reflects competing ideological currents:

1. **Eurasianism:** Russia as a unique Eurasian civilization distinct from both Europe and Asia; advocated by Aleksandr Dugin
2. **Neo-Imperialism:** Recovery of great power status and sphere of influence in former Soviet space
3. **Atlanticism (diminished):** Previously advocated closer Western ties; marginalized after 2014

## 3. Foreign Relations

### Allies & Partners

**Belarus**
- Closest ally; Union State treaty creates economic and political integration
- Hosted Russian nuclear weapons (tactical) since 2023
- Dependent on Russia economically and politically; Lukashenko regime survives through Russian support

**China**
- "Comprehensive strategic partnership" without formal alliance
- Major economic lifeline post-2022 sanctions; China absorbs ~34% of Russian exports
- Military cooperation: joint exercises, technology sharing
- Natural gas supplies via Power of Siberia pipeline
- Tensions exist: China avoids direct military aid in Ukraine; Russia is junior partner economically

**Iran**
- Military cooperation: drone supplies (Shahed/Geran) for Ukraine war
- Technology exchange: Iranian drones for Russian military technology
- Both under Western sanctions; shared opposition to US hegemony
- Strategic coordination in Syria and Middle East

**North Korea**
- Growing military relationship: ammunition and artillery supplies to Russia
- 2024 mutual defense treaty signed
- Labor exports to Russia
- Limited economic ties but strategic significance for munitions

**India**
- Major energy customer (oil imports surged post-2022)
- Longstanding defense relationship (Soviet/Russian arms tradition)
- Strategic autonomy policy maintains ties despite Western pressure
- Balancing act between Russia, US, and China

**CSTO Members (Armenia, Belarus, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan)**
- Military alliance modeled on NATO Article 5 concept
- Collective Rapid Reaction Force for regional interventions
- Limited effectiveness: no CSTO support for Ukraine war; Armenia crisis revealed limitations

**Syria**
- Key Middle Eastern ally; Russian military intervention 2015 saved Assad regime
- Naval facility at Tartus (Mediterranean access)
- Air base at Khmeimim

### Adversaries

**United States**
- Primary geopolitical adversary
- Sanctions regime, diplomatic isolation
- Nuclear arms control breakdown (New START suspended 2023, expired 2026)
- Proxy conflict in Ukraine

**NATO Alliance**
- Existential threat narrative in Russian doctrine
- Expansion from 16 to 32 members since 1991
- Key adversaries: Poland, Baltic states (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania), UK
- Finland and Sweden accession (2023-2024) eliminated neutrality buffer

**European Union**
- Economic sanctions and energy decoupling
- Military support to Ukraine
- Diplomatic isolation strategy

**Ukraine**
- Active state of war since February 2022 invasion
- Territorial claims: annexation of Crimea (2014) and four oblasts (2022)
- Russian narrative frames conflict as "special military operation" against Western aggression

### Key Relationships (Complex)

**Turkey**
- Pragmatic relationship despite NATO membership
- Energy cooperation (TurkStream pipeline)
- Mediation role (grain deal, prisoner exchanges)
- Competing interests in Syria, Caucasus

**Gulf States**
- Saudi Arabia: OPEC+ coordination on oil production
- UAE: Financial hub for sanctions evasion
- Limited political alignment, transactional energy cooperation

## 4. Military Capabilities

### Nuclear Arsenal (World's Largest)

**Total Warheads:** 5,459 (2025 estimate)

**Strategic Nuclear Forces (~2,832 warheads):**
- **Intercontinental Ballistic Missiles (ICBMs):** ~333 missiles carrying 1,254 warheads
  - RS-28 Sarmat (Satan II): New heavy ICBM, 10+ MIRVs, range 18,000 km
  - Topol-M/RS-24 Yars: Road-mobile and silo-based ICBMs
- **Submarine-Launched Ballistic Missiles (SLBMs):** 192 missiles carrying 992 warheads
  - Borei-class submarines (4 active): RSM-56 Bulava missiles
  - Delta IV class: R-29RMU Sineva missiles
- **Strategic Bombers:** 67 aircraft (Tu-160, Tu-95MS) carrying 586 warheads

**Tactical Nuclear Weapons:** ~1,500 warheads (largest tactical arsenal globally)
- Land-based: Iskander-M missiles, artillery
- Sea-based: Torpedoes, anti-ship missiles
- Air-based: Cruise missiles, gravity bombs
- Deployed in Belarus since 2023

**Nuclear Doctrine (2024 Update):**
- Lowered threshold for nuclear use
- Allows nuclear response to conventional attacks that "critically threaten sovereignty"
- Strategic ambiguity maintained on exact triggers
- Nuclear coercion/brandishing tactics employed in Ukraine conflict

### Conventional Forces

**Personnel:** ~900,000 active military (expanded during Ukraine war)

**Ground Forces:**
- ~1,000 tanks (T-90, T-72, T-14 Armata limited)
- Significant losses in Ukraine: estimated 2,500+ tanks destroyed/damaged
- Artillery focus: 4,500+ artillery systems
- Challenges: Logistics, corruption, aging equipment

**Navy:**
- Northern Fleet (Arctic), Pacific Fleet, Baltic Fleet, Black Sea Fleet, Caspian Flotilla
- Submarines: Nuclear-powered attack (Yasen-class), ballistic missile (Borei-class)
- Surface fleet: Aging destroyers, frigates, corvettes
- Black Sea Fleet severely damaged in Ukraine conflict (Moskva cruiser sunk 2022)

**Aerospace Forces:**
- Air superiority fighters: Su-57 (limited), Su-35, Su-30
- Strategic bombers: Tu-160, Tu-95
- Ground attack: Su-34, Su-25
- Air defense: S-400, S-300 systems (exported to multiple countries)

**Special Operations & Intelligence:**
- **SVR (Foreign Intelligence Service):** External intelligence operations
- **FSB (Federal Security Service):** Domestic security, counter-intelligence, some foreign operations
- **GRU (Main Intelligence Directorate):** Military intelligence, special operations (Wagner Group origins)
- Spetsnaz: Special forces units

### Defense Industry

- World's third-largest arms exporter
- Major systems: S-400/S-500 air defense, Su-35 fighters, T-90 tanks, Kalibr missiles
- Sanctions impact: Import substitution programs for critical components
- Artillery production: Sustained high output for Ukraine conflict

### Modernization Status

- **State Armament Program 2018-2027:** Ambitious modernization goals
- **Reality Check:** Ukraine war revealed:
  - Logistics failures
  - Command-and-control issues
  - Equipment losses outpacing production
  - Reliance on older Soviet stockpiles
  - Drone dependency on Iran
  - Ammunition dependency on North Korea

## 5. Economy & Resources

### Overview

Russia has a **high-income mixed economy** heavily dependent on energy exports. Post-2022 sanctions fundamentally restructured trade relationships toward Asia.

**Key Economic Indicators:**
- GDP Growth: 4.1% (2024), slowing to 1.5% (2025), 1.0% (2026f)
- Inflation: 7.9% (2024)
- Unemployment: ~2.6% (historical low due to war mobilization)
- Government Debt: 35.7% of GDP (low by global standards)

### Energy Resources (Strategic Asset)

**Oil:**
- 2nd largest oil producer globally
- 8th largest proven reserves
- Key pipelines: Transneft network, ESPO (to China), Druzhba (to Europe, reduced)

**Natural Gas:**
- **Largest proven reserves globally**
- 2nd largest producer
- Gazprom monopoly on pipeline exports
- Power of Siberia pipeline to China (expanding)
- Nord Stream pipelines destroyed 2022 ( sabotage)
- LNG development (Yamal LNG, Arctic LNG-2)

**Coal:**
- 2nd largest reserves globally
- Major exporter to Asia

**Other Strategic Minerals:**
- Nickel: Significant production (Nornickel)
- Aluminum: Rusal among world's largest
- Rare earths: Substantial untapped reserves
- Diamonds: Alrosa (world's largest producer)
- Gold, platinum, palladium: Major producer
- Uranium: Large reserves

### Key Exports (2024)

- Crude petroleum: Primary export
- Refined petroleum products
- Natural gas
- Coal
- Wheat (world's largest exporter)
- Metals (iron, steel, aluminum, nickel)
- Arms/military equipment
- Fertilizers
- Diamonds

### Major Export Partners (2024)

1. China: 34.6%
2. India: 17.7%
3. EU (combined): ~10%
4. Turkey: 6.93%
5. Kazakhstan/EEU: ~8%

### Major Imports

- Vehicles and automotive parts
- Machinery and electronics
- Pharmaceuticals
- Semiconductors and high-tech components
- Consumer goods

### Major Import Partners (2024)

1. China: 56.6%
2. EU: 16.63%
3. Turkey: 4.23%
4. Kazakhstan: 4.75%
5. India: 2.41%

### Economic Challenges

**Structural Weaknesses:**
- Over-dependence on energy exports (30%+ of federal budget)
- Brain drain: Skilled emigration accelerated post-2022
- Demographic decline: Aging, shrinking population
- Corruption: Ranked 141st on Corruption Perceptions Index
- Sanctions: Technology access restrictions, financial isolation

**War Economy Effects:**
- Military spending driving GDP growth (unsustainable)
- Labor shortages from mobilization
- Inflation pressures
- Western asset seizures (~$300 billion frozen)

### Financial Position

- **Foreign Exchange Reserves:** $606.7 billion (4th largest globally)
- **National Wealth Fund:** Sovereign wealth fund used to cushion sanctions impact
- **Currency:** Ruble volatility managed through capital controls
- **Sanctions Evasion:** Use of yuan, ruble settlements, crypto, third-country intermediaries

## 7. Historical Context

### Russian Empire (1721-1917)
- Imperial expansion across Eurasia
- Defeated in Russo-Japanese War (1904-05)
- World War I collapse led to revolution

### Soviet Era (1917-1991)

**Key Events:**
- 1917: Bolshevik Revolution
- 1922: Soviet Union established
- 1941-45: World War II (Great Patriotic War) - 27 million Soviet dead
- 1947-1991: Cold War with United States
- 1956: Hungarian uprising suppression
- 1968: Prague Spring invasion
- 1979-89: Afghanistan War
- 1985-91: Gorbachev reforms (glasnost, perestroika)
- 1991: Soviet Union dissolution

**Cold War Nuclear History:**
- Peak arsenal: ~46,000 warheads (1975)
- Arms control treaties: SALT, START, INF Treaty
- Nuclear tests: 715 detonations (largest: Tsar Bomba, 50 megatons)
- Biological weapons program (Sverdlovsk anthrax leak 1979)
- Chemical weapons (Novichok nerve agents)

### Russian Federation (1991-Present)

**1990s: Boris Yeltsin Era**
- Economic "shock therapy" and chaos
- Hyperinflation, oligarch rise
- 1993 constitutional crisis
- First Chechen War (1994-96)
- IMF loans and Western integration attempts

**2000-2008: Putin Consolidation**
- Economic recovery from oil boom
- Second Chechen War
- Centralization of power
- Suppression of independent media
- 2008 Georgia War (brief)

**2008-2012: Medvedev Presidency**
- Putin as Prime Minister
- 2008 global financial crisis
- Reset with US attempted
- 2010 Arab Spring responses

**2012-Present: Putin's Third Term and Beyond**
- 2014: Annexation of Crimea, Eastern Ukraine intervention
- 2015: Syria intervention
- 2018-2020: Constitutional reforms
- 2022-Present: Full-scale invasion of Ukraine
