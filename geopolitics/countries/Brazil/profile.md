Brazil is the largest country in South America and the Southern Hemisphere, with the 5th largest land area globally. It is one of only three countries (alongside China and Russia) with land borders with 10+ nations. Its territory spans 16,880 km of land borders, requiring significant defense resources for patrol and sovereignty protection.

### Political Context

**Lula's Third Term (2023-present):** Lula returned to power in January 2023 after defeating incumbent Jair Bolsonaro in a narrow election (50.9% vs 49.1%). His government represents a coalition of left and center-left parties, facing a conservative-dominated Congress.

**Key Policy Priorities:**
- Environmental protection and Amazon deforestation reversal
- Social welfare expansion (revising Bolsa Família)
- Economic growth through infrastructure investment
- Rebuilding international relations strained under Bolsonaro
- Indigenous rights and land demarcation

**Political Challenges:**
- Deeply polarized society post-2022 election
- Conservative Congress blocking progressive legislation
- January 8, 2023 insurrection attempt by Bolsonaro supporters
- Ongoing investigations into coup plots
- Economic inequality (Gini 51.6) and poverty (23% below poverty line)
- Corruption scandals affecting multiple parties

**Government Structure:**
Brazil is a federal presidential republic with 26 states and one federal district. The National Congress consists of the Federal Senate (81 senators) and Chamber of Deputies (513 members). The judiciary is independent, with the Supreme Federal Tribunal (STF) as the highest court.

## 3. Foreign Relations

### Strategic Doctrine: Multi-Alignment

Brazil pursues a **"multi-alignment" strategy** (also called "active non-alignment"), maintaining relationships with competing powers without formal alliances. This approach prioritizes:
- National development interests over ideological blocs
- South-South cooperation
- Regional leadership in South America
- Reform of international institutions to reflect emerging power realities

### Key Relationships

#### **China: Primary Economic Partner**
- **Trade:** China accounts for 28% of Brazilian exports ($348.7B total exports, 2025)
- **Investment:** Major investor in infrastructure, energy, and mining
- **Strategic Dimension:** Brazil's largest trading partner since 2009
- **Tensions:** Limited; Brazil maintains One China policy
- **Lula's Approach:** Seeks balanced engagement, resists decoupling pressure from Washington

#### **United States: Traditional Partner with Frictions**
- **Trade:** 12% of Brazilian exports (third largest partner)
- **History:** Long-standing ally; US supported 1964 coup against João Goulart
- **Bolsonaro Era:** Close alignment with Trump administration
- **Lula Era:** Tensions over democracy concerns, environmental policy criticism
- **Cooperation Areas:** Technology, education, some security cooperation
- **Friction Points:** Venezuela stance, BRICS engagement, Cuba/Iran relations

#### **Argentina: Regional Anchor**
- **Trade:** 4.1% of exports (fourth largest)
- **MERCOSUR:** Founding member with Argentina, Paraguay, Uruguay
- **Political Alignment:** Generally aligned with Brazil on regional integration
- **Economic Challenges:** Argentina's economic instability affects regional trade

#### **Russia: Energy and Strategic Partner**
- **Trade:** 4.1% of imports (fertilizers, oil)
- **BRICS:** Founding member alongside Brazil, India, China, South Africa
- **Ukraine War:** Brazil maintains neutrality, refused sanctions
- **Agricultural Dependence:** Critical fertilizer imports for agribusiness

#### **India: Growing BRICS Partner**
- **Trade:** Growing but still modest
- **BRICS Coordination:** Aligned on Global South priorities
- **Common Ground:** UN Security Council reform, developing country representation
- **Pharmaceuticals:** Important source of generic medicines

#### **European Union: Historic Partner**
- **Trade:** 14.3% of exports (second largest partner)
- **MERCOSUR-EU Trade Agreement:** Negotiated but not ratified (Lula government pushing for completion)
- **Environmental Standards:** EU demands on Amazon protection create negotiation complexity

#### **Africa: Historical and Cultural Ties**
- **Diaspora:** Largest African diaspora outside Africa
- **Lula Focus:** Priority on South-South cooperation
- **CPLP:** Active member of Community of Portuguese Language Countries
- **Investment:** Brazilian companies active in Angola, Mozambique

### Nuclear Capabilities

**Nuclear Weapons:** Brazil does **NOT possess nuclear weapons** and is a signatory to the Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT).

**Nuclear Submarine Program (PROSUB):**
- **Program:** Developing indigenous nuclear-powered attack submarine
- **Name:** Submarino com Propulsão Nuclear (PROSUB)
- **Vessel:** Álvaro Alberto (under construction)
- **Timeline:** First vessel expected operational late 2020s
- **Technology:** French Scorpène-class design adapted for nuclear propulsion
- **Strategic Rationale:** Protect "Blue Amazon" (4.4M km² territorial waters)
- **Partnership:** Technology transfer agreement with France

**Nuclear Facilities:**
- Uranium enrichment capability at Resende
- Under IAEA safeguards
- Strictly civilian nuclear program

## 6. Geopolitical Role

### Regional Position: South American Leader

Brazil is the **natural hegemon** of South America by virtue of:
- Size (47% of South American territory)
- Population (49% of South American population)
- Economy (largest GDP in region)
- Military capabilities

**Regional Integration Projects:**

| Organization | Role |
|--------------|------|
| **MERCOSUR** | Founding member; promotes South American free trade |
| **UNASUR** | Founded with Brazilian leadership (currently weakened) |
| **CELAC** | Active participant |
| **CPLP** | Community of Portuguese Language Countries |

### Global Position: Emerging Power

**BRICS Membership:**
- Founding member (2009)
- Hosts BRICS Bank (New Development Bank) headquarters in São Paulo (though originally proposed for Shanghai; Brazil fought for and got first presidency)
- Key advocate for BRICS expansion (supported Argentina's bid)

**G20 Participation:**
- Active voice on global financial governance
- Advocates for developing country interests

**United Nations:**
- **Security Council Reform:** Brazil seeks a permanent seat
- **G4 Nations:** Coalition with India, Germany, Japan for permanent seats
- **Opposition:** China opposes Japan's bid; regional rivals oppose Brazil's
- **Peacekeeping:** Major troop contributor historically

**Climate Change:**
- **Amazon:** 60% of Amazon rainforest in Brazilian territory
- **Global Importance:** Critical for carbon sequestration, biodiversity
- **Controversy:** Deforestation rates under Bolsonaro drew international criticism
- **Lula Commitment:** Pledged zero deforestation by 2030

### Global South Leadership

Brazil positions itself as:
- **Voice of the developing world** in international forums
- **Bridge between West and Global South**
- **Mediator** in South-South conflicts (e.g., Iran nuclear negotiations under Lula)
- **Alternative development model** advocate

**Lula's Global South Strategy (2023-present):**
- Re-engagement with Africa and Middle East
- BRICS expansion advocacy
- Non-alignment with Western positions on Ukraine/Russia
- Criticism of US dollar hegemony
- Push for multipolar world order

## 7. Historical Context

### Pre-Colonial and Colonial Era (1500-1822)

- **1500:** Portuguese arrival (Pedro Álvares Cabral)
- **Colonial Period:** Sugar, gold, and brazilwood economy
- **Slavery:** 4+ million Africans brought to Brazil (largest in Americas)
- **Independence:** September 7, 1822 (Pedro I declares independence)

### Empire (1822-1889)

- **Pedro I (1822-1831):** Constitutional monarchy established
- **Pedro II (1831-1889):** Long reign; abolition movement grows
- **Paraguayan War (1864-1870):** Largest conflict in South American history; Brazil leads coalition
- **Slavery Abolished:** May 13, 1888 (last nation in Western Hemisphere)

### First Republic (1889-1930)

- **Military coup:** November 15, 1889 ends monarchy
- **Old Republic:** Coffee oligarchy dominance
- **Industrialization:** Early modernization begins

### Vargas Era (1930-1945)

- **1930 Revolution:** Getúlio Vargas takes power
- **Authoritarian State:** Estado Novo dictatorship (1937-1945)
- **WWII:** Brazil joins Allies (1942); Brazilian Expeditionary Force fights in Italy
- **Industrialization:** State-led development model

### Democratic Period (1945-1964)

- **Fourth Republic:** Brief democracy
- **Developmentalism:** Import substitution industrialization
- **Political Instability:** Growing polarization
- **1961-1964:** João Goulart presidency; military opposition grows

### Military Dictatorship (1964-1985)

**The 1964 Coup:**
- **April 1, 1964:** Military overthrows President João Goulart
- **US Support:** Operation Brother Sam; CIA involvement documented
- **Cold War Context:** Anti-communist alignment

**Repression:**
- **Institutional Acts:** Suspended constitutional protections
- **AI-5 (1968):** Most repressive decree; dissolved Congress
- **Torture:** State-sponsored torture of political dissidents
- **Deaths/Disappearances:** 434 confirmed killed/disappeared; 20,000+ tortured
- **Censorship:** Complete media control

**"Brazilian Miracle" (1968-1973):**
- Rapid economic growth (10%+ annual)
- Infrastructure development
- Income inequality worsened

**Transition to Democracy:**
- **1974-1985:** Gradual liberalization (abertura)
- **1982:** First free legislative elections
- **1985:** Civilian president elected
- **1988:** New democratic constitution

### Democratic Era (1985-present)

| President | Years | Key Events |

| **José Sarney** | 1985-1990 | Transition; hyperinflation |

### Key Historical Turning Points

1. **Paraguayan War (1864-1870):** Brazil's largest conflict; established regional dominance
2. **Abolition (1888):** Last Western Hemisphere nation to end slavery
3. **WWII Alliance (1942-1945):** Break from Axis sympathy; alignment with Allies
4. **1964 Coup:** 21-year dictatorship; profound societal impact
5. **1988 Constitution:** Democratic foundation document
6. **2003 Lula Election:** First Workers' Party president; social transformation
7. **2016 Dilma Impeachment:** Political crisis; conservative backlash
8. **2022 Lula Victory:** Return of left after Bolsonaro era; January 8 insurrection
