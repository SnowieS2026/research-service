### Political System

Turkey operates as a **presidential republic** following the 2017 constitutional referendum that abolished the parliamentary system and concentrated executive power in the presidency. The 600-seat Grand National Assembly of Turkey (TBMM) remains the legislative body, but with significantly diminished powers compared to the pre-2017 system.

### The AK Party Era (2002-Present)

The Justice and Development Party (AKP - Adalet ve Kalkınma Partisi) has governed Turkey since 2002, making Erdoğan the dominant political figure for over two decades. Key characteristics:

- **Ideological Orientation:** Conservative-democratic with Islamist roots; AKP rejects Kemalist secularism while embracing an "Anatolian conservative" identity
- **Electoral Dominance:** Won consecutive elections through a combination of economic growth, infrastructure development, and appeals to religious-conservative voters
- **Power Consolidation:** Post-2016 failed coup, extensive purges of military, judiciary, and civil service; crackdown on media and civil society
- **Constitutional Changes:** 2017 referendum transformed Turkey from parliamentary to presidential system, eliminating checks on executive power

### Domestic Challenges

**The Kurdish Issue:**
- An estimated 15-20 million Kurds (Turkey's largest minority) have long sought cultural recognition and political rights
- The PKK (Kurdistan Workers' Party) has waged an insurgency since 1984; conflict has claimed 40,000+ lives
- Erdoğan initially pursued peace talks (2013-2015) but returned to military operations after peace collapsed
- The Kurdish political movement (HDP) has faced severe repression; leaders imprisoned, parties banned
- Kurdish issue directly linked to Syria policy (YPG/PYD ties to PKK)

**Democratic Backsliding:**
- Freedom House rates Turkey as "Not Free" 
- Reporters Without Borders ranks Turkey among the worst for press freedom (165/180)
- Tens of thousands imprisoned post-2016 coup attempt; widespread allegations of torture
- Judicial independence severely compromised; opposition politicians routinely arrested
- Economic pressures increasing (inflation peaked at 85.5% in October 2022; 32.9% as of August 2025)

**2023 Elections:**
- Erdoğan won reelection in May 2023 runoff (52.18% vs. Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu's 47.82%)
- AKP lost parliamentary majority but governs with coalition (MHP and others)
- Opposition remains fragmented; economic dissatisfaction growing but not yet politically decisive

## 3. Foreign Relations

### Strategic Doctrine: "Strategic Autonomy"

Turkey's foreign policy under Erdoğan pursues **strategic autonomy**—maintaining NATO membership and Western ties while cultivating independent relationships with Russia, China, and regional powers. This approach reflects:

- **Neo-Ottoman Vision:** Former Foreign Minister Davutoğlu's "central state" concept envisions Turkey as pivot between Balkans, Caucasus, Middle East, and Mediterranean
- **Multi-alignment:** Engaging both Western and non-Western blocs (NATO, SCO observer, BRICS partner) without exclusive commitments
- **Transactional Diplomacy:** Leverage-based approach seeking concessions from all partners
- **Defense Independence:** Building indigenous defense industry to reduce Western dependency

### United States

**Status:** Complicated ally; NATO membership with severe strains

**Key Issues:**
- **Syria:** Turkey opposes US support for Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF/YPG), which Turkey considers a PKK affiliate
- **F-35/F-16 Dispute:** Turkey's purchase of Russian S-400 systems (2019) led to US expulsion from F-35 program; Biden administration approved F-16 modernization (2023) after Turkey approved Swedish NATO accession
- **Fethullah Gülen:** Turkey demands US extradite cleric accused of orchestrating 2016 coup attempt
- **Sanctions:** CAATSA sanctions imposed 2020 over S-400 purchase
- **Trade:** $43 billion total trade in goods and services (2024); US is 5th largest import source

**Trump Factor:** Second Trump administration (2025-) introduces unpredictability—transactional approach may benefit Turkey through bilateral deals, or may escalate tensions over Syria/Kurdish issues

### Russia

**Status:** Complex partnership; cooperation and competition simultaneously

**Key Dynamics:**
- **Syria:** Opposing aims—Russia backed Assad; Turkey supported rebels—but managed deconfliction through Astana process
- **Defense Cooperation:** S-400 missile purchase (2019) demonstrated willingness to defy NATO; Turkey seeks technology transfer
- **Energy:** Turkey imports significant Russian natural gas; TurkStream pipeline operational
- **Tourism/Trade:** Russia is Turkey's largest import source; Russian tourists critical to Turkish tourism economy
- **Ukraine:** Turkey maintains delicate balance—provides TB2 drones to Ukraine, closes Bosporus to warships, but avoids direct confrontation and maintains economic ties with Russia
- **Black Sea:** Grain deals mediation showcased Turkey's utility as intermediary

**Strategic Value:** Russia relationship gives Turkey leverage with the West while reducing dependency on Western systems

### European Union

**Status:** Candidate country; accession process frozen since 2016

**Relationship Dynamics:**
- **Customs Union:** In effect since 1995; major trade relationship (EU is Turkey's largest trading partner)
- **Migration Deal:** 2016 agreement—Turkey controls migration flows to Europe for €6 billion in assistance
- **Political Conditionality:** EU demands democratic reforms, rule of law, and human rights improvements—unmet under Erdoğan
- **Pragmatic Cooperation:** Despite political divergence, functional cooperation continues on migration, energy, defense industry
- **Defense Role:** Turkey increasingly positioned as external defense partner for EU states (Italy, UK bilateral arrangements)

**2025 Assessment:** Relations described as "managed pragmatism without political convergence"—transactional cooperation without shared vision

### China

**Status:** Growing economic partnership with geopolitical limits

**Key Elements:**
- **Belt and Road:** Turkey is a potential key node in China's BRI; Middle Corridor (Trans-Caspian route) offers alternative to Russian routes
- **Trade:** China major source of imports; Turkey runs significant trade deficit
- **Technology:** Turkey seeks Chinese investment in infrastructure and technology; some 5G discussions
- **Uyghur Issue:** Turkey offers symbolic support for Uyghurs (cultural affinity as Turkic people) but avoids confronting China directly
- **Limits:** Turkey unlikely to pursue SCO membership given NATO commitments; China relationship primarily economic

### Syria

**Status:** Critical national security priority; direct military involvement

**Turkey's Syria Policy:**
- **Assad Opposition:** Turkey supported anti-Assad rebels from 2011; backed various opposition groups including some Islamist factions
- **YPK/PKK Threat:** Primary concern is Kurdish autonomous region in northern Syria; multiple military operations (Operation Euphrates Shield 2016, Olive Branch 2018, Peace Spring 2019)
- **Post-Assad (2024):** Assad regime collapse creates new dynamics—Turkey consolidates influence in north, competes with Israel over Syria's future direction
- **Refugees:** Turkey hosts 4+ million Syrian refugees; domestic pressure for repatriation

**2025 Scenarios:**
1. **Optimistic:** US-Turkey compromise on SDF/YPG; Turkey accepts Kurdish political integration in exchange for PKK separation
2. **Pessimistic:** Military escalation with US-backed forces; Syria becomes arena of proxy conflict

### Iraq

**Status:** Neighbor with complex security relationship

**Key Issues:**
- **PKK Operations:** Turkey conducts regular cross-border operations against PKK bases in northern Iraq
- **Water Disputes:** Tigris-Euphrates water rights; Turkish dams affect Iraqi water security
- **Energy:** Potential for enhanced energy cooperation; Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline
- **Iraqi Kurdistan:** Good relations with KRG (Kurdistan Regional Government); trade and energy partnerships

### Iran

**Status:** Regional rival with limited cooperation

**Dynamics:**
- **Competition:** Opposing sides in Syria (pre-2024), Yemen (supporters of opposite factions)
- **Economic:** Trade continues despite sanctions on Iran
- **Kurdish Issue:** Both oppose Kurdish independence movements
- **Israel:** Turkey opposes Iranian regional influence; competes for leadership in Muslim world

### Israel

**Status:** Severely strained diplomatic relations

**Evolution:**
- **Historical Ally:** Turkey was first Muslim-majority state to recognize Israel (1949); close military and economic ties
- **Erdoğan Era:** Relations deteriorated over Gaza conflicts, Turkey's Hamas support, Mavi Marmara incident (2010)
- **2023-2025:** Israel-Gaza war severely damaged relations; Turkey sharply critical of Israeli operations
- **Syria Competition:** Post-Assad, Turkey and Israel may compete for influence in new Syria

### Gulf States (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar)

**Status:** Reconciled after period of tension

**Dynamics:**
- **2017-2021 Rift:** Qatar blockade; Turkey supported Qatar against Saudi-UAE bloc
- **Reconciliation:** Erdoğan's visit to Saudi Arabia (2022) began normalization
- **Economic Cooperation:** Gulf investment in Turkey increased; defense partnerships emerging
- **Regional Influence:** Turkey competes with Gulf states for regional leadership

### Azerbaijan

**Status:** "One nation, two states" — closest ally

**Relationship:**
- **Ethnic Affinity:** Turkic peoples; strong cultural bonds
- **Military Support:** Turkey provided decisive support in Second Nagorno-Karabakh War (2020)
- **Energy:** Southern Gas Corridor transports Azerbaijani gas to Europe via Turkey
- **Strategic Alignment:** Turkey gains influence in Caucasus; Azerbaijan gains powerful patron

### Balkans

**Status:** Historical sphere of influence; active engagement

**Approach:**
- **Ottoman Heritage:** Cultural and religious connections with Muslim populations
- **Soft Power:** Turkish schools, media, business investment
- **Security:** Defense agreements with Albania, Bosnia; peacekeeping contributions
- **EU Competition:** Turkey offers alternative to EU integration for some Balkan states

### Africa

**Status:** Major focus of expansion (2000s-present)

**Presence:**
- **Diplomatic:** Embassies across Africa expanded dramatically
- **Defense Industry:** Major customer for Turkish drones and military equipment
- **Development:** TIKA (development agency) active in infrastructure, humanitarian assistance
- **Military Bases:** Base in Somalia; security partnerships in Sahel, Horn of Africa
- **Competition:** Turkey competes with China, France, Russia for influence

## 4. Military Capabilities

### Overview

The Turkish Armed Forces (Türk Silahlı Kuvvetleri - TSK) are the **second-largest military in NATO** after the United States, with approximately **481,000 active personnel** and 380,000 reserves. Since 2016, Turkey has pursued aggressive indigenous defense industrialization.

### Force Structure

| Branch | Active Personnel | Key Assets |
|--------|-----------------|------------|
| **Land Forces** | ~260,000 | 2,200+ tanks, 8,500+ armored vehicles, artillery systems |
| **Naval Forces** | ~45,000 | 140+ vessels, submarines (Gür-class), frigates, landing ships |
| **Air Force** | ~50,000 | 300+ combat aircraft, F-16s (primary), planned Kaan indigenous fighter |

### Budget and Expenditure

- **Defense Budget:** $25 billion (2024) — 17th globally, 2.09% of GDP
- **Defense Exports:** $10+ billion (2025) — doubled from ~$7 billion in 2024
- **Export Growth:** Defense exports surged 100%+; customers include African, Asian, and increasingly European states

### Nuclear Arsenal

**Status:** Turkey hosts nuclear weapons but possesses none independently

- **Incirlik Air Base:** Approximately 50 U.S. B61 nuclear gravity bombs stored under NATO nuclear sharing arrangement
- **No Independent Program:** Turkey is NPT signatory; no known nuclear weapons program
- **Deterrence:** Relies on NATO extended deterrence; growing debate about strategic independence

### Key Strategic Assets

**Drone Program:**
- **Bayraktar TB2:** Combat-proven UAV used extensively in Ukraine, Libya, Nagorno-Karabakh; export success to 30+ countries
- **Akıncı:** Heavy combat UAV with advanced capabilities
- **Kızılelma:** Jet-powered UAV in development

**Missile Systems:**
- Indigenous ballistic missile program (Bora, Khan missiles)
- Long-range cruise missile development
- Air defense systems: S-400 (Russian, purchased 2019), domestic Hisar series

**Naval:**
- Landing helicopter dock (TCG Anadolu) — can deploy UAVs
- Submarine force: 6 diesel-electric submarines, building Reis-class (Type 214)
- Indigenous frigate program (İstanbul-class)

**Indigenous Fighter Program:**
- **Kaan (TF-X):** Fifth-generation fighter in development; first flight 2023
- Engine dependence: Initial engines from foreign sources; indigenous engine development ongoing

### Defense Industry Achievements

Turkey has transformed from defense importer to major exporter:

| Company | Products |
|---------|----------|
| **Baykar** | Bayraktar TB2, TB3, Akıncı UAVs |
| **TAI** | Kaan fighter, Hurjet trainer, Gökbey helicopter |
| **Aselsan** | Electronics, radar, communications systems |
| **Roketsan** | Missiles, rockets, artillery systems |
| **FNSS** | Armored vehicles |
| **BMC** | Armored vehicles, tactical wheeled vehicles |

### Key Dependencies

- **Engines:** Critical gap in indigenous propulsion (aircraft engines, naval turbines)
- **Advanced Electronics:** Some components still imported from Western suppliers
- **Air Defense:** Gap until S-400 operational and domestic systems mature

### Military Operations

**Active Deployments:**
- **Northern Syria:** Operations against ISIS and YPG/PKK (ongoing since 2016)
- **Northern Iraq:** Regular operations against PKK bases
- **Libya:** Military support to GNA (2019-2020); ongoing military advisory presence
- **Qatar:** Military base; regional power projection
- **Somalia:** Training base; counter-piracy, counterterrorism
- **Azerbaijan:** Training and advisory role post-2020 war
- **NATO:** Balkans, Afghanistan (until 2021)

## 5. Economy & Resources

### Overview

Turkey is the **17th largest economy globally** with a GDP of $1.32 trillion (2024). It is an OECD and G20 member, classified as an emerging market with upper-middle-income status. Economic growth averaged 5.4% from 2002-2022, doubling income per capita and reducing poverty from 20%+ to 7.6%. However, recent years have seen significant macroeconomic challenges.

### Key Economic Indicators

| Indicator | Value (2024) |

| GDP (Nominal) | $1.32 trillion |
| GDP Growth | 3.2% (2024), projected 3.1% (2025) |
| Inflation | 32.9% (August 2025) — peaked at 85.5% (Oct 2022) |
| Central Bank Rate | 40.5% (September 2025) |
| Exchange Rate | ~41 TL/USD (2025) |
| Unemployment | ~10% |
| Youth Unemployment | ~20% |

### Major Sectors

**Industry (Manufacturing):**
- **Automotive:** Major production hub; Ford, Toyota, Renault, Fiat, Hyundai factories; exports to EU
- **Defense:** Rapidly growing; drones, armored vehicles, naval vessels
- **Textiles/Apparel:** Traditional strength; exports to EU and Middle East
- **Electronics/Appliances:** White goods manufacturing (Arçelik, Vestel)

**Services:**
- **Tourism:** 50+ million visitors annually pre-COVID; recovering; critical for foreign currency earnings
- **Finance/ Banking:** Growing Islamic finance sector

**Agriculture:**
- **Major Products:** Wheat, barley, cotton, hazelnuts (world's largest producer), fruits, vegetables
- **Employment:** ~15% of workforce
- **Water Resources:** Critical constraint; climate change impacts

**Energy:**
- **Dependency:** Net energy importer; ~70% of energy needs imported
- **Natural Gas:** Pipeline imports from Russia, Azerbaijan; LNG imports
- **Oil:** Limited domestic production; imports from Iraq, Russia, others
- **Renewables:** Expanding solar and wind; hydropower significant
- **Nuclear:** First nuclear plant (Akkuyu) under construction (Russian-built)

**Major Import Products:**

### Economic Challenges

1. **Inflation:** Chronic high inflation erodes purchasing power; central bank tightening cycle (rates peaked at 50% in March 2024)
2. **Currency Depreciation:** Lira lost significant value; 41 TL/USD in 2025 vs. ~3 TL/USD in 2017
3. **Current Account Deficit:** Persistent; dependent on capital flows and tourism
4. **External Debt:** Corporate and banking sector exposure to foreign currency debt
5. **Brain Drain:** Skilled emigration to Europe and North America
6. **Earthquake Impact:** February 2023 earthquakes caused $81.5 billion in damage; 50,000+ deaths; 70% of population in seismic zones
7. **Structural Issues:** Low productivity growth, declining FDI, need for structural reforms

### Critical Minerals & Resources

- **Boron:** World's largest boron reserves (72% of global); critical for electronics
- **Marble/Stone:** Significant exports
- **Chromium:** Major producer
- **Rare Earths:** Limited known reserves; dependency on imports
- **Strategic Position:** Energy transit corridor (pipelines from Caucasus/Central Asia to Europe)

## 7. Historical Context

### Ottoman Empire Legacy

- **Peak:** 16th-17th century (Suleiman the Magnificent); empire spanned Europe, Asia, Africa
- **Decline:** 19th century; "Sick Man of Europe"
- **World War I:** Allied with Central Powers; defeated 1918
- **Collapse:** Partition by Allied powers (Treaty of Sèvres)

### Republic Foundation (1923)

- **Mustafa Kemal Atatürk:** Founded modern Turkey from remnants of Ottoman Empire
- **War of Independence (1919-1923):** Expelled Greek, French, Armenian forces; established borders
- **Secularism:** Abolished caliphate, Islamic law; adopted European legal codes, Latin alphabet
- **Westernization:** Oriented toward Europe; secular state ideology

### Cold War (1945-1991)

- **NATO Membership (1952):** Joined to counter Soviet threat
- **Truman Doctrine:** US military and economic aid to contain communism
- **Cyprus (1974):** Turkish invasion partitioned island; ongoing dispute with Greece
- **Coup Era:** Military coups in 1960, 1971, 1980; military as "guardian of Kemalism"

### Post-Cold War (1990s-2002)

- **Economic Instability:** High inflation, weak coalition governments
- **Kurdish Conflict:** PKK insurgency intensified
- **EU Aspirations:** Customs Union with EU (1995); candidate status (1999)

### AK Party Era (2002-Present)

**Reforms and Growth (2002-2013):**
- Economic growth: GDP tripled; poverty reduction
- EU reform package: Strengthened civilian control of military, expanded rights
- Democratic opening: Brief rapprochement with Kurdish movement

**Authoritarian Turn (2013-Present):**
- **Gezi Protests (2013):** Brutal crackdown; erosion of democratic image
- **Corruption Scandal (2013):** Purge of prosecutors and police
- **Presidential System (2017):** Constitutional referendum concentrated power
- **Failed Coup (2016):** Massive purges; 150,000+ dismissed or arrested
- **Economic Challenges:** Currency crisis (2018, 2022); high inflation
- **Foreign Policy Shifts:** Syria intervention, S-400 purchase, confrontation with allies

### Major Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |

| 1952 | NATO membership | Western alignment solidified |
| 1974 | Cyprus invasion | Ongoing dispute with Greece; EU accession barrier |
| 1999 | EU candidate status | Reform trajectory set |

| 2023 | Erdoğan reelected | Continuation of AKP rule despite economic crisis |
