## 2. Leadership & Politics

### Supreme Leader
**Ayatollah Ali Khamenei** (born 1939, in power since 1989)
- Ultimate authority over all branches of government
- Commander-in-chief of armed forces
- Controls IRGC and Basij paramilitary
- Appoints head of judiciary, heads of state media, half of Guardian Council
- Final say on all foreign policy and nuclear matters
- Health concerns since 2014; succession remains opaque

### President (Current)
**Masoud Pezeshkian** (took office August 2024)
- Reformist-backed candidate who defeated hardliner Saeed Jalili in runoff
- Former Health Minister under Khatami (2001-2005)
- First president to engage in direct military confrontation with Israel (2024 exchanges)
- Platform: domestic reform, nuclear negotiation revival, economic relief
- Constrained by Supreme Leader and hardliners in parliament

### Key Power Centers

| Institution | Head | Role |
|--------------|------|------|
| **Guardian Council** | 6 clerics + 6 jurists | Vetoes legislation, screens candidates |
| **Expediency Discernment Council** | Appointed by Supreme Leader | Resolves parliament-Guardian disputes |
| **Assembly of Experts** | 88 clerics | Theoretically can select/remove Supreme Leader |
| **Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC)** | Hossein Salami (commander) | Military, intelligence, economic empire |
| **Ministry of Intelligence** | Appointed by President (w/Leader approval) | Domestic surveillance, foreign intelligence |
| **Parliament (Majlis)** | 290 members | Legislation (subject to Guardian Council veto) |

### Political Factions
- **Principalists/Conservatives**: Back theocratic system, oppose Western engagement, dominate judiciary and IRGC
- **Reformists**: Seek domestic liberalization, nuclear diplomacy; weakened since 2009 crackdown
- **Moderates**: Pragmatists (e.g., Rouhani) favoring engagement within system constraints
- **Ultraconservatives**: Reject negotiations entirely; Raisi/Khamenei loyalists

4. **Revolutionary legitimacy:** 1979 revolution narrative central to regime's claim to rule
5. **War trauma:** Iran-Iraq War created military doctrine of self-reliance and asymmetric deterrence

## 8. Current Priorities & Strategy 2025-2026

- **Primary objective:** Maintain theocratic system against domestic and external threats
- **Internal security:** IRGC and Basij suppress dissent; sophisticated surveillance apparatus
- **Succession:** Khamenei's eventual death creates leadership vacuum; potential power struggle

### Nuclear Strategy

| Position | Status |
|----------|--------|

| **De facto stance** | Threshold capability; breakout option preserved |
| **JCPOA** | Demands full sanctions relief before return to compliance |
| **Negotiations** | Stalled since 2022; limited engagement with European intermediaries |

### Regional Positioning

- **Syria post-Assad:** Major setback; land bridge to Hezbollah disrupted

- **Hamas:** Severely degraded; limited remaining value as proxy
- **Yemen:** Houthi attacks on shipping give Iran leverage

### Economic Priorities

1. **Sanctions evasion:** Expand oil exports to China, develop illicit networks
2. **Petrochemicals:** Shift focus to harder-to-sanction derivatives
3. **Import substitution:** Military self-sufficiency, domestic production

### Military Modernization

- Ballistic missile production continues despite sanctions
- Drone exports to Russia (Shahed-136/Geran-2) generate revenue
- Air defense improvements (Bavar-373 domestic system)
- Naval asymmetric capabilities (fast boats, mines in Hormuz)
