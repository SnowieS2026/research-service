## 1. Basic Facts

### Geography & Demographics
- **Official Name:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Capital:** Abuja (since December 1991; moved from Lagos)
- **Largest City:** Lagos (~15+ million metro area, one of world's fastest-growing cities)
- **Area:** 923,768 km² (356,669 sq mi) — 32nd largest country globally
- **Population:** ~223.8 million (2023 estimate) — **Africa's most populous nation** and 6th most populous globally
- **Population Growth:** ~2.4% annually; projected to reach 400+ million by 2050
- **Ethnic Groups:** 250+ ethnic groups; Hausa-Fulani (29%), Yoruba (21%), Igbo (18%), Ijaw (10%), Kanuri (4%), others (18%)
- **Languages:** 500+ languages; English (official), Hausa, Yoruba, Igbo (major regional languages)
- **Religions:** Muslim (~50%, predominantly north), Christian (~40%, predominantly south), Traditional/other (~10%)
- **Life Expectancy:** ~54 years
- **Literacy Rate:** ~62%
- **HDI:** 0.548 (161st globally, "low" category)
- **Time Zone:** UTC+1 (West Africa Time)

### Political Structure
- **Government Type:** Federal presidential republic
- **Constitution:** 1999 constitution (4th republic; amended 2010)
- **Administrative Divisions:** 36 states + Federal Capital Territory (Abuja)
- **Independence:** October 1, 1960 (from United Kingdom)
- **National Anthem:** "Arise, O Compatriots"
- **Currency:** Nigerian Naira (NGN, ₦)

- **Age:** Born March 29, 1952 (currently in his 70s)

**Vice President:** Kashim Shettima (since May 29, 2023)

### Political Landscape

**Major Parties:**
- **All Progressives Congress (APC):** Center-right, ruling party since 2015; coalition of former opposition parties
- **People's Democratic Party (PDP):** Center, main opposition; ruled 1999-2015
- **Labour Party:** Left-leaning, surged in 2023 elections behind Peter Obi
- **New Nigeria People's Party (NNPP):** Regional strength in Kano State

**Political Dynamics:**
- Deep regional/ethnic divisions: North vs. South; Muslim vs. Christian
- Power rotation convention: "Zoning" between North and South, Muslim and Christian

2. Security: Counter-insurgency in northeast, banditry in northwest, separatism in southeast

- 2023 election was highly contested; Tinubu won with 36.6% of vote
- Opposition disputes legitimacy; claims of irregularities by INEC (Independent National Electoral Commission)
- Regional tensions: Strong Labour Party showing in southeast; APC strength in north and southwest

## 3. Foreign Relations

### Strategic Orientation
Nigeria operates as Africa's **hegemonic power in West Africa** and pursues:
- **Concentric Circles Doctrine:** Immediate neighbors → West Africa → Africa → Global
- **Non-alignment tradition:** Historically balanced between East and West
- **African leadership:** Largest contributor to UN peacekeeping in Africa; dominant ECOWAS power
- **Economic diplomacy:** Protecting oil revenues; seeking investment diversification

### Key Bilateral Relationships

#### United States
- **Status:** Strategic partner; security cooperation partner
- **Framework:** US-Nigeria Binational Commission (established 2010); Non-NATO Major Non-NATO Ally status considered
- **Military Cooperation:** US provides training, intelligence sharing, equipment for counter-Boko Haram operations; AFRICOM engagement
- **Trade:** US is 4th largest import partner (8.63% of imports); Nigeria receives AGOA (African Growth and Opportunity Act) benefits
- **Tensions:** Human rights concerns over Nigerian security forces; US travel advisories for Nigeria; disputes over intellectual property
- **2024-2025 Context:** Tinubu visited US in September 2023; seeking increased investment in energy and tech sectors

#### China
- **Status:** Major economic partner; infrastructure financier
- **Trade:** China is Nigeria's **largest import source** (33.6% of imports); exports to China include crude oil, minerals
- **Infrastructure:** Belt and Road Initiative participant; Chinese loans fund rail projects (Lagos-Ibadan, Abuja-Kaduna), airports, ports
- **Debt Concerns:** Nigeria owes billions to China; debt sustainability concerns as naira devalued
- **Investment:** Chinese companies in telecommunications (Huawei), construction, mining
- **Strategic Balance:** Nigeria maintains Taiwan relations while engaging Beijing carefully

#### United Kingdom
- **Status:** Historical colonial power; close cultural and diplomatic ties
- **Commonwealth:** Key member; British influence in legal system, education, military training
- **Trade:** Significant UK investment in oil/gas, banking, consumer goods
- **Security:** UK military training and intelligence cooperation; counter-terrorism support
- **Diaspora:** Large Nigerian diaspora in UK (~200,000+); significant remittances

#### France
- **Status:** Growing security and economic partner
- **Context:** France's traditional West African sphere (former colonies) borders Nigeria; increasing cooperation on Sahel security
- **Trade:** France imports Nigerian LNG; French companies (TotalEnergies, Vinci) invested in energy and construction
- **Military:** France operates in Sahel (Operation Barkhane wound down 2022; French forces redeployed); Nigeria coordinates on regional security

#### Russia
- **Status:** Limited but growing engagement
- **Developments:** 2023-2024 saw Russia-Africa Summit engagement; potential arms deals discussed
- **Nuclear:** Rosatom discussions on nuclear power development
- **Grain:** Nigeria imports wheat; affected by Ukraine war-related food price inflation

### ECOWAS Leadership

**Role:** Nigeria is the **dominant power** in the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS)
- **Population weight:** Nigeria accounts for ~50% of ECOWAS population
- **Military contributions:** Largest troop contributor to ECOWAS standby force
- **Economic influence:** Largest economy in ECOWAS by GDP

**2023-2024 Niger Crisis:**
- Following the July 2023 military coup in Niger, Tinubu (as ECOWAS Chair) threatened military intervention
- ECOWAS imposed sanctions on Niger junta
- Nigeria cut electricity supplies to Niger
- Nigeria ultimately **did not intervene militarily** — domestic opposition, military capacity concerns, and regional diplomacy prevailed
- Resolution: Prolonged diplomatic standoff; Niger remains under military junta as of 2025
- **Implications:** Exposed limits of Nigerian/ECOWAS hard power; Sahel region increasingly unstable (coups in Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Guinea)

**Regional Security Architecture:**
- **Multinational Joint Task Force (MNJTF):** Nigeria, Chad, Cameroon, Niger, Benin — counter-Boko Haram
- **Gulf of Guinea Security:** Nigeria leads anti-piracy efforts; coordinates with international navies

### International Organizations

- **United Nations:** Founding member; regular Security Council aspirant; major peacekeeping contributor
- **African Union:** Key member; financial contributor
- **ECOWAS:** De facto leader; provides ~50% of budget
- **OPEC:** Member since 1971; major oil exporter
- **Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC):** Controversially joined under Babangida; Nigeria has large Muslim population
- **Commonwealth of Nations:** Active member; English-speaking heritage
- **G77:** Member; developing world advocate
- **World Trade Organization:** Member
- **African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA):** Signatory; potential economic transformation

## 4. Military Capabilities

### Overview
Nigeria maintains **West Africa's largest military** and one of Africa's most capable armed forces, though effectiveness is hampered by corruption, equipment issues, and multi-front security challenges.

### Nigerian Armed Forces

**Personnel:** ~223,000 active duty
- **Army:** ~135,000 (largest component)
- **Navy:** ~25,000
- **Air Force:** ~18,000
- **Paramilitary:** ~30,000 (including Mobile Police, Customs, Immigration)
- **Reserves:** Estimated 30,000+ (mobilization capacity limited)

**Defense Budget:** ~$2.5-3.0 billion USD annually (official; actual spending likely higher with off-budget security allocations)
- **% of GDP:** ~0.8-1.0% (relatively low for a country facing multiple insurgencies)
- **Corruption issues:** Procurement scandals endemic; ghost soldiers on payroll

### Nigerian Army

**Organization:** 6 divisions + Guards Brigade
- **1st Division:** Kaduna (Northwest)
- **2nd Division:** Ibadan (Southwest)
- **3rd Division:** Jos (Northcentral)
- **6th Division:** Port Harcourt (Niger Delta)
- **7th Division:** Maiduguri (Northeast — primary counter-Boko Haram)
- **8th Division:** Sokoto (Northwest — banditry operations)
- **Guards Brigade:** Abuja (presidential protection)

**Major Equipment:**
- **Main Battle Tanks:** T-72 (various sources), VT-4 (Chinese), T-55 (legacy)
- **APCs/IFVs:** BTR-80/82, BMP-1/2, Steyr 4K 7FA, various Chinese types
- **Artillery:** Chinese and Russian systems; 122mm/152mm howitzers
- **Small Arms:** AK variants, FN FAL, locally produced rifles

**Operational Experience:**
- Civil War (1967-1970): Learned hard lessons in counter-insurgency
- Liberia/Sierra Leone (1990s): ECOMOG peace enforcement
- Boko Haram (2009-present): 15+ years of counter-insurgency
- Niger Delta: Oil infrastructure protection

### Nigerian Air Force

**Personnel:** ~18,000
**Aircraft Inventory:**
- **Combat Aircraft:**
  - JF-17 Thunder (Chinese-Pakistani; ~12 acquired 2021+)
  - F-7 Airguard (Chinese MiG-21 variant; being replaced)
  - Alpha Jet (German-French; training/light attack)
  - Super Tucano (Brazilian; 12 acquired 2021 for counter-insurgency)
- **Helicopters:**
  - Mi-35/Mi-24 (Russian attack helicopters)
  - AW109 (Italian utility)
  - Bell 412 (US utility)
- **Transport:**
  - C-130 Hercules (US; aging fleet)
  - ATR 42/72 (French-Italian)

**Capabilities:** Air superiority limited; focused on close air support and transport for counter-insurgency

### Nigerian Navy

**Personnel:** ~25,000
**Mission:** Maritime security in Gulf of Guinea; protect offshore oil infrastructure; counter-piracy

**Fleet:**
- **Frigates:** 2 (Chinese P18N class)
- **OPVs:** Several coastal patrol vessels (Chinese, French, Dutch)
- **Fast Attack Craft:** Multiple missile boats and patrol craft
- **Helicopters:** AgustaWestland AW109, AW101

**Gulf of Guinea Operations:**
- Partners with international navies (US, EU, China) on anti-piracy
- Deep Blue Project: Maritime security initiative launched 2021

### Strategic Assets

**Nuclear Status:** None — Non-nuclear weapon state; NPT signatory
- **Research Reactor:** Nigerian Research Reactor-1 (NIRR-1) at Ahmadu Bello University (Chinese-supplied, 2010)
- **Nuclear Ambitions:** Expressed interest in civil nuclear power; discussions with Rosatom, China

**Intelligence Services:**
- **NIA:** National Intelligence Agency (external)
- **DSS:** Department of State Services (internal security, counter-terrorism)
- **DMI:** Defence Intelligence Agency (military intelligence)

**Cyber Capabilities:** Limited; cyber security command established but under-resourced

### Security Challenges (Multi-Front)

#### 1. Boko Haram / ISWAP (Northeast)
- **Status:** Active insurgency since 2009
- **Groups:** Boko Haram (JAS); Islamic State West Africa Province (ISWAP)
- **Territory:** Borno, Yobe, Adamawa States; Lake Chad region
- **Casualties:** 350,000+ total deaths (combatants + civilians); 2+ million displaced
- **Kidnappings:** Chibok girls (2014); Dapchi girls (2018); ongoing abductions
- **Nigerian Military Response:** Operation Lafiya Dole; MNJTF coordination
- **Current Status:** Insurgency degraded but not defeated; attacks continue

#### 2. Banditry / Kidnapping (Northwest/Central)
- **Status:** Widespread criminal violence
- **Groups:** "Bandits" — armed criminal networks; some with ideological/ethno-religious dimensions
- **States:** Zamfara, Katsina, Kaduna, Niger, Sokoto
- **Casualties:** Thousands killed annually; mass kidnappings of students, villagers
- **Economic Impact:** Mining operations disrupted; agricultural production affected

#### 3. Niger Delta Militancy (South-South)
- **Status:** Low-level; historical militancy (2000s peak)
- **Groups:** Niger Delta Avengers, various militant groups
- **Grievances:** Oil revenue sharing; environmental degradation
- **Government Response:** Amnesty program (2009+); federal development commissions
- **Threat Level:** Moderate; potential for escalation if grievances not addressed

#### 4. IPOB/ESN Separatism (Southeast)
- **Status:** Active low-level insurgency
- **Groups:** Indigenous People of Biafra (IPOB); Eastern Security Network (ESN)
- **Grievances:** Igbo marginalization; Biafra independence
- **Government Response:** Military operations; designation as terrorist organization
- **Risk:** Potential for wider ethnic conflict

### Defense Procurement & Partnerships

**Major Suppliers:**
- **China:** Tanks, APCs, drones, aircraft (JF-17, drones)
- **Russia:** Helicopters, small arms, air defense
- **United States:** Super Tucano, training, intelligence sharing (restricted by Leahy Law human rights conditions)
- **Pakistan:** JF-17 co-production
- **UAE:** Logistics hub for weapons procurement
- **Turkey:** Drones, naval vessels

**Domestic Industry:**
- **DICON:** Defence Industries Corporation (small arms, ammunition)
- **Shipbuilding:** Naval shipyard in Lagos
- **Maintenance:** Limited; dependence on foreign support

## 5. Economy & Resources

### Macro Overview

**GDP (2025):**
- **Nominal:** $285 billion (52nd globally)
- **PPP:** $2.254 trillion (19th globally) — significant purchasing power difference reflects low local prices
- **GDP Growth:** 3.9% (2025), 4.2% (2026 projected), 4.0% (2027 projected)
- **GDP per capita (Nominal):** ~$1,200 (173rd globally)
- **GDP per capita (PPP):** ~$9,500 (135th globally)

**GDP Composition:**
- **Services:** 54.4%
- **Industry:** 23.6%
- **Agriculture:** 22.0%

**Key Economic Indicators:**
- **Inflation:** 33.2% (2024 estimate) — one of world's highest; food inflation even higher
- **Unemployment:** 22.6% (Q1 2025); youth unemployment ~40%+
- **Poverty Rate:** 31.2% below $2.15/day; 48.8% below $3.65/day (2025 estimates)
- **Gini Coefficient:** 35.1 (medium inequality)
- **External Debt:** $85.9 billion (2020)
- **Foreign Reserves:** $38.77 billion (2017)

### Oil & Gas Sector

**Overview:** Nigeria is Africa's **largest oil producer** and a founding OPEC member. The petroleum sector dominates exports and government revenues, though contributes only ~9% of GDP.

**Oil Reserves:** ~37 billion barrels proven (9th globally)
**Production:** ~1.5-1.7 million bpd (pre-Tinubu: 2+ million bpd)
**Exports:** Petroleum accounts for ~90% of export earnings and ~60% of government revenue

**Key Oil Fields:**
- **Onshore:** Niger Delta (Bonny, Escravos, Forcados)
- **Offshore:** Deep-water (Agbami, Bonga, Erha)

**Major Companies:**
- **NNPC Ltd.** — National oil company; joint ventures with IOCs
- **International Oil Companies:** Shell, ExxonMobil, Chevron, TotalEnergies, Eni
- **Independents:** Various Chinese, Indian, indigenous companies

**Challenges:**
- **Oil theft:** Estimated 200,000-400,000 bpd lost to theft and sabotage
- **Infrastructure:** Pipelines, terminals aging; underinvestment
- **OPEC quotas:** Production limited by OPEC+ agreements
- **Refining:** Domestic refineries largely non-functional; Dangote Refinery (2023+) aims to change this

**Natural Gas:**
- **Reserves:** 5.9 trillion cubic meters (9th globally)
- **LNG Exports:** Nigeria LNG (NLNG) major exporter
- **Domestic Use:** Power generation (chronic supply issues)
- **Flaring:** Historically high; efforts to reduce for environmental and economic reasons

### Solid Minerals & Mining

**Overview:** Underdeveloped sector; Nigeria has significant mineral resources but limited commercial exploitation

**Major Minerals:**
- **Iron Ore:** Large deposits at Itakpe; Ajaokuta Steel Company (never fully operational)
- **Coal:** Enugu fields; historically significant but production minimal
- **Tin/Columbite:** Jos Plateau; production declined since 1960s peak
- **Limestone:** Extensive; for cement production
- **Gold:** Zamfara, Niger, Osun States; artisanal mining + some commercial operations
- **Rare Earth Elements:** Potential in several states
- **Uranium:** Exploration ongoing

**Mining Sector Contribution:** <1% of GDP
**Reform Efforts:** 2007 Minerals and Mining Act; attempts to attract foreign investment

### Agriculture

**Overview:** Employs ~30% of workforce; contributes ~22% of GDP; was once food exporter but now imports significant food

**Major Crops:**
- **Roots & Tubers:** Yam, cassava, sweet potato (Nigeria is world's largest cassava producer)
- **Cereals:** Sorghum, millet, maize, rice (rice imports significant despite domestic production)
- **Cash Crops:** Cocoa, palm oil, rubber, cotton (historically major exports)
- **Other:** Groundnuts (peanuts), beans, vegetables

**Livestock:**
- **Cattle:** 19 million head (largest in Africa); significant pastoralist-farmer conflicts
- **Poultry:** 42% of households own chickens
- **Fish:** Aquaculture growing; imports still significant

**Challenges:**
- Low productivity (subsistence methods predominant)
- Climate change impacts on Sahel agriculture
- Farmer-herder conflicts over land/water
- Infrastructure: Poor storage, roads, markets

### Industrial & Services Sectors

**Manufacturing:**
- **Cement:** Dangote Cement (largest in Africa), BUA, Lafarge
- **Food Processing:** Flour, sugar, beverages
- **Textiles:** Historically significant but declined due to imports
- **Automobiles:** Assembly plants (Honda, Toyota, others); limited local manufacturing
- **Dangote Group:** Conglomerate with cement, flour, sugar, oil refinery operations

**Financial Services:**
- **Banking:** Major banks (GTBank, Access, Zenith, UBA, First Bank); consolidated post-2005 reforms
- **Fintech:** Growing sector (Flutterwave, Paystack acquired by Stripe for $200M)
- **Capital Markets:** Nigerian Stock Exchange; limited depth

**Technology:**
- **Lagos:** "Yabacon Valley" tech hub
- **Startups:** Fintech, agritech, logistics; significant VC investment pre-2023
- **Challenges:** Infrastructure (power, internet), talent flight

**Nollywood:** Film industry; 2nd largest by output globally after Bollywood

### Trade Partners

**Exports (2022): $42.4 billion**
1. India (14.8%)
2. Spain (11.3%)
3. China (5.99%)
4. Netherlands (5.27%)
5. South Africa (5.12%)
6. France (4.65%)

**Imports (2022): $52 billion**
1. China (33.6%) — dominant supplier
2. Netherlands (8.82%)
3. United States (8.63%)
4. India (6.65%)
5. Belgium (3.82%)

**Trade Balance:** Structural deficit (imports exceed exports); worsened by naira devaluation

### Economic Challenges

**Currency Crisis:**
- **Naira Devaluation:** Tinubu's 2023 float led to dramatic depreciation; ~50%+ loss
- **Multiple Exchange Rates:** Official vs. parallel market rates; arbitrage opportunities
- **Removal of Fuel Subsidy:** Ended decades of subsidies; caused fuel price spike (from ~65 naira/liter to 600+ naira/liter)

**Debt:**
- External debt service consumes ~30% of government revenue
- Domestic debt also significant
- Risk of debt distress as naira depreciation increases debt burden

**Infrastructure Deficit:**
- **Power:** ~4-5 GW installed capacity for 220+ million people; chronic blackouts
- **Transportation:** Roads poor; railways limited
- **Port Congestion:** Lagos ports inefficient; delays cost billions

**Corruption:**
- Transparency International CPI score: 25/100 (145th/180)
- Oil sector particularly affected: theft, corruption, ghost contracts
- Procurement fraud in defense sector

**Structural Issues:**
- Import-dependent economy (refined petroleum, food, machinery)
- Limited industrialization
- Youth unemployment crisis
- Informal economy dominates (~50%+ of employment)

## 6. Geopolitical Role

### Regional Power

Nigeria is **West Africa's hegemon** and **Africa's most consequential state** by demographic and economic weight:

- **Population:** 1 in 4 Africans is Nigerian
- **Economy:** Largest GDP in Africa (nominal); South Africa and Egypt compete on PPP
- **Military:** Largest military in sub-Saharan Africa
- **Diplomacy:** Dominant voice in ECOWAS, influential in AU

### Africa's Superpower Aspirations

Nigeria has long sought permanent UN Security Council representation for Africa and positions itself as a Great Power in waiting. However:

**Strengths:**
- Demographic weight
- Oil wealth
- Cultural influence (Nollywood, Afrobeats, literature)
- Regional military hegemony

**Weaknesses:**
- Domestic instability
- Economic underperformance
- Infrastructure deficit
- Corruption
- Education/health outcomes poor

### International Organization Leadership

**ECOWAS:** Nigeria provides:
- ~50% of ECOWAS budget
- Leadership on regional crises (though Niger exposed limits)
- Military backbone of regional interventions

**African Union:**
- Financial contributor (though in arrears at times)
- Leadership roles in AU Commission
- Advocates for African solutions to African problems

**United Nations:**
- Major peacekeeping contributor historically (Liberia, Sierra Leone, Darfur, others)
- Seeks permanent Security Council seat
- Voting pattern: Non-aligned, Global South solidarity

**OPEC:**
- Member since 1971
- Often acts as swing producer within OPEC
- Coordinates production with Saudi Arabia, others

### Cultural Influence

**Soft Power:**
- **Nollywood:** 2nd largest film industry globally; exports across Africa and diaspora
- **Music:** Afrobeats global phenomenon (Burna Boy, Wizkid, Davido)
- **Literature:** Nobel laureate Wole Soyinka; Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie; Chinua Achebe (Things Fall Apart)
- **Diaspora:** Large communities in US, UK, South Africa; remittances significant (~$17-20 billion annually)

### Maritime Security

**Gulf of Guinea:**
- Nigeria's coastline is critical for West African trade
- Piracy historically significant (though declining 2022-2024)
- International partnerships: US, EU, China all naval presence

## 7. Historical Context

### Pre-Colonial Era

**Major Empires/Kingdoms:**
- **Kanem-Bornu Empire (700s-1900):** Northeast; Islamic influence; controlled trans-Saharan trade
- **Hausa City-States (1000s-1800s):** Northern Nigeria; major trading centers (Kano, Katsina, Zaria)
- **Oyo Empire (1400s-1830s):** Yoruba; controlled much of southwest; collapsed after internal strife and Fulani jihad
- **Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903):** Largest pre-colonial state; founded by Usman dan Fodio's jihad; Islamic theocracy
- **Benin Kingdom (1100s-1897):** Edo people; famous for bronze artwork; fell to British invasion
- **Igbo City-States:** Decentralized political structure in southeast

### Colonial Era (1900-1960)

**British Rule:**
- 1914: Amalgamation of Northern and Southern Protectorates (previously administered separately)
- **Indirect Rule:** Frederick Lugard's system using traditional rulers (especially in north)
- **Economic Extraction:** Palm oil, cocoa, groundnuts, tin, coal
- **Railway Construction:** Linked interior to coast for resource extraction

**Colonial Legacy:**
- **North-South Divide:** Deliberately separate administration created lasting divisions
- **Christianity vs. Islam:** Missionary activity in south; north remained predominantly Muslim
- **Education:** South had higher education rates; north lagged (elite resistance)
- **Nationalism:** Emerged post-WWII; Nnamdi Azikiwe, Obafemi Awolowo, Ahmadu Bello

### Independence & First Republic (1960-1966)

- **October 1, 1960:** Independence from Britain
- **1963:** Republic declared; Nnamdi Azikiwe becomes President
- **Parliamentary System:** Federal with three regions (North, West, East)
- **Political Parties:** NPC (North), NCNC (East), AG (West) — ethnic/regional bases
- **Instability:** 1964-65 election crises; Western Region violence ("Wild Wild West")

### Military Era & Civil War (1966-1979)

**January 1966 Coup:**
- Major Kaduna Nzeogwu leads coup
- Prime Minister Tafawa Balewa, Northern Premier Ahmadu Bello, Western Premier Samuel Akintola killed
- Perceived as "Igbo coup" by northern officers

**July 1966 Counter-Coup:**
- Northern officers overthrow Ironsi (Igbo head of state)
- Yakubu Gowon becomes head of state
- Anti-Igbo pogroms in north; ~30,000+ killed

**Biafran War (1967-1970):**
- **May 30, 1967:** Colonel Odumegwu Ojukwu declares Eastern Region independent as Republic of Biafra
- **Causes:** Ethnic violence against Igbos; marginalization; oil resources
- **International Involvement:**
  - **UK/US/Soviet Union:** Supported Nigeria (federal side)
  - **France/Ivory Coast/Gabon/Zambia:** Recognized/supported Biafra
- **Blockade:** Nigeria imposed starvation blockade; humanitarian crisis
- **Atrocities:** Both sides committed war crimes
- **January 1970:** Biafra surrenders; estimated **1-3 million deaths** (mostly civilians from starvation/disease)
- **Legacy:** "No victor, no vanquished" policy; but Igbo marginalization persists

### Second Republic (1979-1983)

- **1979:** Return to civilian rule; Shehu Shagari (NPN) elected president
- **American-style presidential system** replaced parliamentary
- **Oil boom:** 1970s oil prices funded infrastructure expansion
- **Corruption:** Widespread; economic mismanagement
- **1983:** Shagari re-elected amid fraud allegations

### Military Rule & Transition (1983-1999)

**Buhari Regime (1983-1985):**
- Coup leader; strict anti-corruption campaign
- War Against Indiscipline (WAI)
- Authoritarian; press restrictions

**Babangida Regime (1985-1993):**
- Structural Adjustment Programme (SAP): IMF/World Bank reforms
- Political transition program; parties banned then allowed
- 1993 election: Moshood Abiola (SDP) won; Babangida annulled results
- Joined OIC (Organisation of Islamic Cooperation) — controversial

**Abacha Regime (1993-1998):**
- Brutal dictatorship
- Human rights abuses; executions (Ken Saro-Wiwa, others)
- Looting: Billions stolen; still being recovered
- International isolation

**Abubakar Transition (1998-1999):**
- Abacha's death; General Abdulsalami Abubakar oversees transition
- 1999: Election of Olusegun Obasanjo (PDP)

### Fourth Republic (1999-Present)

**Obasanjo (1999-2007):**
- Former military ruler (1976-1979) elected civilian president
- Economic reforms: Banking consolidation, telecom liberalization, debt relief
- Anti-corruption: EFCC established
- Security: Niger Delta militancy (2004-2009); amnesty program
- 2003, 2007 elections: Controversial

**Yar'Adua (2007-2010):**
- Died in office; illness from 2009
- Niger Delta Amnesty: Reduced militancy
- Good governance agenda limited by health

**Jonathan (2010-2015):**
- Vice President; succeeded Yar'Adua
- 2011 election: Contested by Buhari supporters; post-election violence in north
- Boko Haram escalation: 2014 Chibok kidnapping gained international attention
- Economy: Growth but corruption perceptions worsened

**Buhari (2015-2023):**
- Former military ruler (1983-1985) elected on anti-corruption platform
- Security: Boko Haram degraded but not defeated; banditry emerged
- Economy: 2016 recession (oil price crash); COVID-19 impact
- Criticism: Selective anti-corruption; nepotism; slow reform

**Tinubu (2023-Present):**
- Contested election; 36.6% of vote
- Bold economic reforms: Fuel subsidy removal, naira float
- Security challenges persist on multiple fronts
- Economic hardship from reforms

## 8. Current Priorities & Strategy (2025-2026)

### Economic Stabilization

**Tinubu's Reform Agenda:**
1. **Fuel Subsidy Removal (May 2023):**
   - Ended decades of subsidies costing $5-10 billion annually
   - Fuel prices tripled; protests, strikes threatened
   - Palliative measures: Cash transfers, transportation support (limited reach)

2. **Naira Float (June 2023):**
   - Ended multiple exchange rate system
   - Naira depreciated 50%+ immediately
   - Aim: Attract foreign investment, resolve forex shortages
   - Consequence: Imported inflation; hardship for consumers

3. **Tax Reform:**
   - Proposed tax bills to increase revenue collection
   - VAT increases contested by states

4. **Debt Management:**
   - Seeking restructuring of external debt
   - Engaging IMF, World Bank for support
   - Balancing fiscal consolidation with development needs

**Dangote Refinery (2023):**
- World's largest single-train refinery (650,000 bpd)
- Potential to end refined fuel imports (currently ~100% imported)
- Petrochemical and fertilizer plants co-located
- Game-changer for trade balance if successful

### Security Strategy

**Northeast (Boko Haram/ISWAP):**
- Continue Operation Lafiya Dole with MNJTF
- Challenge: Territorial control regained but insurgency continues asymmetric attacks
- 2024-2025: ISWAP faction gaining relative strength vs. Boko Haram

**Northwest (Banditry/Kidnapping):**
- Aerial bombardments, military operations
- Challenge: Criminal networks embedded in communities
- Negotiation attempts with some groups; mixed results

**Southeast (IPOB/ESN):**
- Military operations; designation as terrorist
- Challenge: Grievances not addressed; risk of escalation

**Niger Delta:**
- Relative calm; amnesty program continuation
- Risk: Oil theft (200,000+ bpd) undermines economics
- Environmental degradation unresolved

### Foreign Policy Priorities

1. **Regional Leadership:**
   - Maintain ECOWAS coherence (tested by Niger crisis)
   - Counter Russian influence in Sahel (Wagner in Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger)
   - Support regional integration (AfCFTA implementation)

2. **Strategic Partnerships:**
   - Balance China (infrastructure financing) vs. US/EU (security cooperation)
   - UK: Post-Brexit trade, diaspora engagement
   - UAE/Turkey: Defense procurement, investment

3. **Energy Diplomacy:**
   - OPEC coordination
   - LNG export expansion
   - Attract investment for gas infrastructure

### Domestic Challenges

**Corruption:**
- EFCC prosecutions continue but perceived as selective
- Whistleblower protection limited
- Oil sector opacity remains

**Infrastructure:**
- Power sector: Privatization failed to deliver; transmission bottleneck
- Transportation: Railway modernization (Chinese loans)
- Ports: Lekki Deep Sea Port (2023) aims to relieve Lagos congestion

**Social Tensions:**
- EndSARS protests (2020): Police brutality, youth unemployment
- Farmer-herder conflicts: Climate + ethnic dimensions
- Religious tensions: Sharia law in northern states (12 states)

## 9. Notable Ties to Other Countries

### United States
- **Type:** Strategic partnership
- **Key Interests:** Security (counter-terrorism), energy (oil/LNG), investment
- **Frictions:** Human rights concerns (Leahy Law restrictions), visa restrictions (past), intellectual property
- **Trade:** AGOA beneficiary; $5+ billion annual trade
- **Diaspora:** ~400,000+ Nigerian-Americans; highly educated demographic
- **Military:** US training, equipment (Super Tucano deal); AFRICOM engagement

### United Kingdom
- **Type:** Historical, cultural, economic
- **Legacy:** Colonial ruler; legal system based on English common law
- **Trade:** UK investment in oil/gas, banking, consumer goods
- **Diaspora:** ~200,000+ in UK; doctors, professionals; significant remittances
- **Education:** Many Nigerian elites educated in UK
- **Commonwealth:** Active participation

### China
- **Type:** Economic partnership, infrastructure financier
- **Investment:** Belt and Road; railways, ports, airports, power
- **Trade:** Largest import source; exports oil to China
- **Debt:** Billions owed to China; concerns about dependency
- **Strategic:** Nigeria maintains relations with Taiwan while engaging Beijing
- **Technology:** Huawei telecom infrastructure; cybersecurity concerns

### France
- **Type:** Security and economic partner
- **Context:** France's sphere in West Africa (former colonies) borders Nigeria
- **Trade:** TotalEnergies invested in Nigerian oil/gas
- **Security:** Sahel coordination (post-Barkhane reorganization)
- **Cultural:** French taught in schools; Alliance Française presence

### South Africa
- **Type:** Continental rival and partner
- **Relationship:** Competition for African leadership; cooperation in AU
- **Xenophobia:** Attacks on Nigerians in South Africa (2015, 2019)
- **Trade:** MTN (telecom) largest investor; Shoprite, other retailers
- **Diplomatic:** At times tense; both claim continental leadership

### Ghana
- **Type:** Regional partner, historical tensions
- **History:** Expulsions of Nigerians/Ghanaians (1969, 1983)
- **Current:** Good relations; ECOWAS cooperation
- **Economic:** Trade, regional integration

### Cameroon
- **Type:** Neighbor, territorial disputes resolved
- **Bakassi Peninsula:** Disputed territory; ICJ ruled for Cameroon (2002); Nigeria withdrew (2008)
- **Security:** Cooperation on Boko Haram (MNJTF)
- **Anglophone Crisis:** Nigeria hosts Cameroonian refugees; mediation efforts

### Niger
- **Type:** Neighbor, security partner, now strained
- **Cooperation:** MNJTF against Boko Haram; Niger Delta militancy cooperation
- **2023 Crisis:** Niger coup; Tinubu (ECOWAS Chair) threatened intervention; ultimately did not intervene
- **Current:** Strained relations; Niger under junta; border tensions
- **Economic:** Nigeria supplies electricity to Niger (cut post-coup)

### Israel
- **Type:** Limited but existing relations
- **History:** Established 1960; broke during 1973 oil embargo; re-established 1992
- **Trade:** Agriculture technology, security equipment
- **Pilgrimage:** Nigerian Christians visit Israel

### India
- **Type:** Economic partner, energy importer
- **Trade:** Largest export destination for Nigerian oil (14.8%)
- **Pharmaceuticals:** Major supplier to Nigeria
- **Diaspora:** Indians in Nigeria business community
